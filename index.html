✍️ Anatomy of a Good First Message

The biggest mistake new users make is writing vague messages. Compare these two approaches:

Vague prompt

Write me an email about our new product.

Claude will produce something generic because it doesn't know your product, audience, tone, or goal.

Clear prompt using Role + Task + Context + Format

Includes specific role, detailed context about audience and features, format requirements for tone and length, and a clear call-to-action request.

The difference in output quality is dramatic.

You are a B2B marketing copywriter.

Write a product launch announcement email for our project management
tool, TaskFlow Pro.

Context:
- Target audience: IT directors at mid-size companies (500-2000 employees)
- Key feature: AI-powered resource allocation that reduces project delays by 30%
- Launch date: March 15
- Special offer: 60-day free trial for companies that sign up in March

Format:
- Subject line + email body
- Professional but approachable tone
- Under 200 words
- End with a clear call-to-action

The four parts of a good prompt are:

1
Role — Tell Claude who it should be ("You are a B2B marketing copywriter")
2
Task — What you need done ("Write a product launch announcement email")
3
Context — Background information Claude needs (audience, details, constraints)
4
Format — How the output should look (length, tone, structure)

You don't always need all four, but the more you include, the better your results.

💡 What Claude Can and Cannot Do

Claude is great at:

  • Drafting and editing text (emails, blog posts, social copy, reports)
  • Summarizing long documents
  • Brainstorming ideas
  • Restructuring information into different formats
  • Analyzing text you provide (competitor copy, survey responses, meeting notes)
  • Explaining concepts in simple terms

Claude has limitations:

  • No real-time data — Claude's training has a knowledge cutoff. Use web search for current information, or paste in the data yourself.
  • No memory across conversations — Each new chat starts fresh. Claude doesn't remember what you discussed yesterday (unless you use Projects — covered in Day 5).
  • Can generate plausible-sounding but incorrect information — Always verify specific facts, statistics, quotes, and claims. Claude is a drafting partner, not a fact database.
  • Works best with your raw material — Don't ask Claude to guess about your product, audience, or data. Paste it in.

Day 2: Prompting — Getting Exactly What You Need

Master specificity, context loading, and iterative refinement to go from "okay output" to "exactly what I wanted."

⏱ ~45 minutes

🪜 The Specificity Ladder

Most people write prompts at Level 1. Your goal is to reach Level 3 consistently.

Level 1 — Vague

Write me a follow-up email.

Result: Generic, could be for anyone, about anything. You'll spend more time editing than you saved.

Level 2 — Directed

Write a follow-up email to a prospect who attended our webinar
on AI-powered analytics but didn't book a demo.

Result: Better — Claude knows the scenario. But it still guesses at tone, length, and approach.

Level 3 — Fully Specified

Write a follow-up email to a marketing director at a mid-size
e-commerce company. She attended our webinar "5 Ways AI Saves
Marketing Teams 10 Hours Per Week" three days ago but didn't
book a demo.

Tone: helpful and consultative, not pushy
Length: under 150 words
Include: one specific insight from the webinar topic, a soft CTA
to book a 15-minute call (not a full demo)
Avoid: "just following up," "I hope this email finds you well,"
multiple CTAs, aggressive urgency

Reference: our best-performing outreach emails use short
paragraphs, lead with value, and end with a low-commitment question.

Result: Claude produces something close to send-ready on the first attempt.

Every detail you add saves you an editing round. Specificity up front is faster than iteration afterward.

📂 Context Loading

Claude doesn't know your brand, your data, or your writing style — unless you give it that information. The fastest way to get high-quality output is to paste in your existing materials.

Paste text directly

Here is our brand voice guide:
[Paste 2-3 paragraphs from your style guide]

Using this voice, write a LinkedIn post announcing our new
partnership with DataCorp.

Upload files

Click the paperclip icon to attach PDFs, CSVs, images, or documents. Claude will read the full file. Good files to upload:

  • Brand or style guides
  • Product one-pagers
  • Competitor landing pages (for analysis)
  • Spreadsheets with campaign data
  • Meeting transcripts or call recordings

The reference pattern

Here is my reference material:
[Paste content]

Now, using this as context, please [your task].

This pattern works every time. The more relevant context you provide, the less guessing Claude does.

🚫 Negative Instructions and Guardrails

Telling Claude what NOT to do is just as important as telling it what to do. This is especially useful when Claude keeps producing patterns you don't want.

Useful negative instructions

  • "Do not use jargon or acronyms."
  • "Do not exceed 150 words."
  • "Do not start with 'In today's fast-paced world' or any cliche opener."
  • "Do not use exclamation marks."
  • "Do not include a greeting or sign-off — just the body text."
  • "Do not mention competitors by name."

Setting boundaries on format

  • "Use bullet points, not numbered lists."
  • "No more than 3 paragraphs."
  • "Do not use headers or subheaders — this is a plain email."

If Claude keeps doing something you don't like, add a specific negative instruction about it. "Stop using the word 'leverage'" is more effective than "use simpler language."

🔁 Iterative Refinement

You don't need a perfect prompt on the first try. Claude is designed for back-and-forth conversation. The skill is knowing how to give useful feedback.

Effective refinement phrases

  • "Keep everything but make the opening more direct."
  • "This is 80% there. Specifically, change the CTA to be less aggressive."
  • "Rewrite just the second paragraph to focus on ROI."
  • "Make it 30% shorter without losing the key points."
  • "The tone is too formal — make it conversational, like a colleague sending a Slack message."
  • "Add a specific example in the third bullet point."

Ineffective feedback

  • "Make it better." (Better how?)
  • "I don't like it." (What specifically?)
  • "Try again." (Same prompt = similar output)

Effective feedback

  • "Make the opening more direct."
  • "Change the CTA to be less aggressive."
  • "Rewrite the second paragraph to focus on ROI."

When to continue vs. start over

  • Continue when the structure and direction are right but details need adjusting
  • Start a new chat when the fundamental approach is wrong and you want to try a completely different angle
  • Start a new chat if the conversation is getting very long (10+ back-and-forth messages) — Claude can lose focus in extremely long threads

💎 Workflow: Prompt-Refine-Polish

A three-step process for consistently getting publish-ready output from Claude. Use this every time you need something more polished than a quick first draft.

Step 1: Draft Your Prompt

Use the Role + Task + Context + Format structure from Day 1:

[ROLE — who should Claude be?]

[TASK — what do you need?]

Context:
- [RELEVANT DETAIL 1]
- [RELEVANT DETAIL 2]
- [RELEVANT DETAIL 3]

Format:
- Tone: [TONE]
- Length: [LENGTH]
- Must include: [REQUIREMENTS]
- Must avoid: [EXCLUSIONS]

Step 2: Pre-Send Checklist

Before hitting Enter, scan your prompt for these five elements. Every one you include improves the output.

  • Tone — Did I specify how it should sound?
  • Length — Did I set a word count or size constraint?
  • Audience — Did I name who will read this?
  • Avoidances — Did I say what NOT to do?
  • Example or reference — Did I provide a sample, style guide, or existing content to match?

If you've checked 3 or more, send it. If fewer than 3, add more detail — it's faster to specify up front than to iterate later.

Step 3: Refine with Targeted Phrases

After Claude's first response, use these phrases to dial in the output:

When you want to... Say this
Adjust length "Make it [30% shorter / under X words / longer with more detail]."
Fix tone "Make the tone more [casual / formal / urgent / empathetic / confident]."
Fix a section "Keep everything, but rewrite the [first paragraph / CTA / headline] to be more [specific quality]."
Add something "Add [a specific stat / a customer quote / a deadline] to the [section]."
Remove something "Remove the [section / sentence about X]. It's not relevant."
Get alternatives "Give me 3 alternative versions of the [headline / subject line / CTA]."
Shift audience "Rewrite this for a [CFO / technical / executive] audience instead."
Final polish "Review this for grammar, clarity, and flow. Fix any issues and list what you changed."

Two rounds of refinement is typical. If you're on round 4+, it's usually faster to start a new chat with a better initial prompt — incorporating what you learned from the failed attempts.

Day 2 Key Takeaways

  • Climb the specificity ladder — Level 3 prompts (with tone, length, audience, avoidances, and references) produce dramatically better output than vague requests.
  • Load context for brand-matched output — Paste in existing copy, style guides, or reference materials so Claude writes in your voice, not a generic one.
  • Use negative instructions as guardrails — Telling Claude what NOT to do is just as powerful as telling it what to do.
  • Iterate with specific feedback — "Rewrite the CTA to be softer" beats "make it better" every time. Two targeted rounds typically get you to publish-ready.

🔄 The Draft-Critique-Revise Loop

This three-step technique consistently outperforms asking Claude for a single draft. It works because Claude is surprisingly good at critiquing its own work.

1
Generate a draft with full context (using what you learned in Days 1-2).
2
Ask Claude to critique its own draft:
Now review this draft as a senior marketing editor. Identify specific
weaknesses in:
- Clarity (any confusing or vague phrases?)
- Persuasion (is the value proposition compelling?)
- Flow (does it read smoothly from start to finish?)
- CTA (is the call-to-action clear and motivating?)

Be specific — point to exact sentences that need improvement.
3
Ask Claude to revise based on its critique:
Now rewrite the draft, fixing every issue you identified. Keep what's
working and improve what isn't.

The result is typically 50-70% better than the first draft. This loop works for emails, blog posts, landing pages, case studies — any content where quality matters.

✅ Do's and Don'ts

The 10 most common mistakes new Claude users make, and what to do instead.

# DO DON'T
1 Paste in existing content as context — brand guides, previous emails, competitor copy, raw notes. Claude works best with your actual materials. Expect Claude to know your brand voice, product, or audience without examples. It can't read your mind or your website unless you paste it in.
2 Ask Claude to critique its own output — "Review this as a senior editor and identify weaknesses." Then ask it to revise based on its critique. Accept the first draft without review. The Draft-Critique-Revise loop produces significantly better results than a single pass.
3 Give specific constraints — tone, word count, audience, required elements, and things to avoid. More detail = better output. Write one-sentence prompts for complex tasks. "Write me a blog post" will get you generic content. Specificity is the single biggest lever.
4 Verify factual claims — statistics, dates, quotes, competitor information, and any specific claims. Always check before publishing. Treat Claude's output as researched fact. Claude can generate plausible-sounding but incorrect information. You are the fact-checker.
5 Iterate with specific feedback — "Make the CTA softer" or "Cut the second paragraph" tells Claude exactly what to change. Give vague feedback like "make it better" or "try again." Claude needs to know what specifically isn't working to fix it.
6 Upload files for long documents — use the paperclip icon for PDFs, CSVs, and documents. Claude reads the entire file. Manually summarize a 20-page document before pasting it in. Just upload the file and let Claude read the original.
7 Use Projects for recurring work — set up custom instructions and knowledge files once, then every conversation starts with that context. Re-explain your brand, audience, and product in every single conversation. That's what Projects are for.
8 Build reusable prompt templates — when a prompt works well, save it with [PLACEHOLDER] variables so you can use it again. Write every prompt from scratch. Most of your tasks fall into recurring categories. Templatize what works.
9 Use negative instructions — "Do not use jargon," "Do not start with a cliche," "Do not exceed 150 words." Hope Claude avoids bad patterns on its own. If you don't want something, say so explicitly.
10 Share your best prompts with teammates — contribute to a shared prompt library so the whole team benefits. Keep effective prompts to yourself. Siloed usage means the team learns slower and produces inconsistent output.

📖 Glossary

Plain-language definitions of terms you'll encounter when using Claude.ai.

Artifact
A standalone content piece (document, table, code, etc.) that Claude generates in a separate panel next to the conversation. Artifacts can be copied, downloaded, shared, and edited through follow-up messages.
Attachment
A file you upload to a conversation using the paperclip icon. Claude can read PDFs, Word documents, CSVs, images, and other file types.
Claude Code
A command-line tool for software developers that lets Claude read and write code directly on a developer's computer. Included in Team and Enterprise plans but used by technical teammates, not in the web interface.
Compliance API
Enterprise-level tools that let IT administrators monitor and audit Claude usage across the organization. Not something most users interact with directly.
Context Window
The total amount of text Claude can "see" in a single conversation, including your messages, Claude's responses, uploaded files, and Project instructions. Team/Enterprise plans have a 200,000-token window — roughly 500 pages.
Conversation
A single chat thread with Claude. Each conversation is independent — Claude doesn't remember other conversations unless you're using a Project with shared context.
Cowork
A mode where Claude works on longer tasks autonomously in the background, like assigning work to a colleague. You describe the task, Claude works on it, and notifies you when it's ready for review.
Custom Instructions
Text you write in a Project that shapes Claude's behavior in every conversation within that Project. Used to define role, audience, voice, constraints, and other persistent context.
Extended Thinking
A capability where Claude spends more time reasoning through complex problems before responding. Results in higher quality output for difficult analytical or strategic tasks.
Hallucination
When Claude generates information that sounds plausible but is factually incorrect. This is why you should always verify specific claims, statistics, and dates in Claude's output.
Knowledge File
A document uploaded to a Project that Claude can reference in every conversation within that Project. Common examples: brand guides, product docs, persona documents.
Model
The specific version of Claude you're using. Sonnet is faster for everyday tasks; Opus is the most capable for complex reasoning. You can select the model using the dropdown in the message box.
MCP (Model Context Protocol)
A system that lets Claude connect to external tools and services like Google Calendar, Asana, or Slack. Available on Team and Enterprise plans.
Prompt
The message you send to Claude. A well-structured prompt includes a role, task, context, and format specification. Better prompts produce dramatically better output.
Project
A persistent workspace in Claude.ai with custom instructions and knowledge files. Every conversation within a Project starts with the same context, eliminating the need to re-explain your brand, audience, and goals.
Role Prompting
A technique where you tell Claude who to be (e.g., "You are a senior marketing editor") to shape its writing style and perspective. One of the most effective simple techniques.
Starred Conversation
A conversation you've pinned for easy access using the star icon. Useful for frequently referenced chats.
Tokens
The units Claude uses to measure text. Roughly 1 token = 0.75 words. Token limits determine how much text fits in a conversation or Project.
Web Search
Claude's ability to search the internet for current information during a conversation. Enabled by default on Team and Enterprise plans.
